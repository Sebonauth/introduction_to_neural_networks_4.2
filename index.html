Starting with the Familiar

Let's begin our journey with something you already know well. Picture trying to predict house prices in your neighborhood. You've collected data about house sizes and their selling prices, and you're ready to build a linear regression model.

Continue

In its simplest form, you know that linear regression gives you an equation that looks like this:

\[ y = mx + b \]

where you're trying to predict the price (y) based on the size (x). The slope (m) tells you how much the price increases for each additional square foot, and the intercept (b) might represent the basic value of the land.

Build Your Vocab

Linear Regression

A statistical method that models the relationship between variables using a straight line. It's like drawing the 'best fit' line through your data points.

Continue

You also know that finding the perfect values for m and b isn't just guesswork. We use the 'sum of squared errors' to find the best line:

\[ \text{Error} = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - (mx_i + b))^2 \]

Stop and Think

Before we move on, why do you think we square the errors instead of just adding them up?

Squaring does two clever things: it makes negative errors positive (so they don't cancel out), and it punishes big mistakes more than small ones. It's like telling our model 'being way off is really bad!'
